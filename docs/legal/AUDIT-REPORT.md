## 1. TOP RISKS (ranked)

### 🔴 #1 — Marketing says cleaners are "background-checked," "EU-insured," and "eco-certified." They are not. (CRITICAL)
**Why it matters:** Your only real verification is Stripe Identity — an ID match. It is **not** a criminal-background check, **not** insurance verification, **not** an eco-certification. Stating these as facts (on the homepage TrustTicker, About page, How-It-Works, and even auto-generated marketing emails) is a deceptive-advertising violation on **both** sides of the Atlantic (FTC Act §5 + state UDAP in the US; UCPD + German UWG in the EU). Worse: it is a **safety claim**. If a cleaner steals from or injures a client, the gap between "background-checked, insured" and "we only checked their ID" becomes the plaintiff's entire case, and it shreds your "we're just a marketplace, not liable" defense.
**What to do:** Stop saying these *today*. Replace with the literal truth: "Identity-verified via Stripe Identity." Remove "background-checked"/"insured"/"eco-certified" from TrustTicker.tsx, HeroSection.tsx, the About copy, and the AI email BRAND_CONTEXT until each is actually performed and documented per cleaner. A disclaimer in the Terms **cannot** rescue a false homepage headline — the marketing itself must change.

### 🔴 #2 — Your cookie banner is fake. Analytics tracking runs no matter what the user clicks. (CRITICAL — EU)
**Why it matters:** The Umami analytics script loads on every public page based only on an env variable — never on consent. Clicking "Decline" does literally nothing. No code reads the stored consent before loading tracking. This is the single most-enforced ePrivacy failure in the EU (prior consent is required before *any* non-essential tracking), and German DPAs treat it as an automatic violation.
**What to do:** Do not inject the Umami script until the user opts in (`consent.analytics === true`, default = deny). Give the banner equal-weight "Accept all" / "Reject all" buttons plus a "Manage" option, and a persistent "Cookie settings" link in the footer to withdraw later.

### 🔴 #3 — Recurring bookings auto-charge the saved card with no auto-renewal consent. (CRITICAL — US)
**Why it matters:** Your cron job silently re-charges customers each cycle (`off_session: true`). That is a "negative-option" subscription, and the FTC's Click-to-Cancel Rule + state Auto-Renewal Laws (California, NY, Illinois, Virginia…) require clear pre-billing disclosure, a *specific* affirmative consent to the recurring charge, and easy online cancellation. You capture none of it. Violations are **per-charge**, a favourite of class actions and AGs, and non-compliant charges can be treated as unconditional gifts (full refund exposure).
**What to do:** Add a distinct checkbox at recurring setup ("I authorize DORIXÉ to charge [amount] every [frequency] until I cancel"), log it with a timestamp, send a reminder before each charge, and provide one-click cancel in the dashboard.

### 🔴 #4 — No US Terms at all. German law / Berlin courts imposed on everyone. (CRITICAL — US/EU)
**Why it matters:** Your Terms force every user into Berlin courts under German law. A US court will ignore that for a US consumer. With no arbitration clause and no class-action waiver, you are fully exposed to US class actions (worker misclassification, biometric/photo claims, false-advertising on the claims in #1, etc.). This is your single largest litigation exposure.
**What to do:** Split the governing-law clause. US users → a chosen US state + (optionally) an individual-arbitration clause with class-action waiver and 30-day opt-out. EU consumers → keep their right to sue in their home country under their own law (you cannot override this). Make it all severable.

### 🔴 #5 — No US privacy notice, no "Do Not Sell or Share" link. (CRITICAL — US)
**Why it matters:** Your privacy policy is GDPR-only. US state laws (CCPA/CPRA in California, plus CO/VA/CT and others) require a "Notice at Collection," a categories table, consumer rights (know/delete/correct/opt-out), and — because you run analytics that can count as "sharing" for advertising — a "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link and honouring the GPC browser signal. You collect *home-interior photos and precise location* from US users, which is high-harm data. California penalties run $2,500–$7,500 per violation.
**What to do:** Add a "U.S. State Privacy Rights" section (or a /legal/privacy-us page linked in the footer) with the categories table, rights, two request methods, and a non-discrimination statement. Add the "Do Not Sell or Share" footer link wired to actually suppress trackers, OR confirm-and-document that no sale/share occurs and keep that statement accurate.

### 🔴 #6 — No 14-day right of withdrawal; your cancellation tiers are unlawful as written. (CRITICAL — EU)
**Why it matters:** EU consumers have a 14-day cooling-off right on services bought online. Your tiers (>48h 100% / 24–48h 50% / <24h 0%) ignore it entirely. As coded, a customer who books 10 days out and cancels the next day is legally owed a **100% refund** regardless of your tier. And because checkout never captures the customer's express request to start the service early, a consumer can withdraw and demand a full refund **even after the clean is done**. Failing to inform them extends the window up to 12 months.
**What to do:** Add a Right of Withdrawal clause + the statutory Model Withdrawal Form. At checkout, for any sub-14-day booking, capture an explicit "begin early and I waive my withdrawal right once performed" checkbox and store it on the booking. Reframe your tiers as a *contractual* policy that only applies *after* the statutory right lapses.

### 🔴 #7 — False green claims, including a verifier ("EcoAudit EU") that may not exist. (HIGH→CRITICAL)
**Why it matters:** Live copy says "offsets 100% … Verified by EcoAudit EU," "neutralise this service," "non-toxic," "100% eco-certified," "No greenwashing — we verify the certifications." A **named third-party certification that doesn't exist** is among the most serious deceptive-claim violations under both FTC Green Guides and the EU Green Claims regime. If eco-levels are self-declared by cleaners, every one of these is misleading.
**What to do:** Drop "Verified by EcoAudit EU" unless a real audit exists. Qualify offset claims (what/how-much/which provider/standard). Replace blanket "non-toxic" with specific substantiated attributes. Relabel self-declared tiers as "DORIXÉ Eco Level: self-declared" and stop using "certified"/"verified." Keep substantiation files.

In Germany a fake/blank Impressum is itself a "warning-letter" (Abmahnung) magnet that competitors can pursue, and it means the legally required controller identity is missing from your Privacy Policy too (which hides it behind "available on request" — not allowed).

### 🔴 #9 — Your subprocessor list is wrong, and you don't disclose international transfers. (HIGH — EU/US)
**Why it matters:** The policy lists "Cloudflare R2" — which you no longer use (you moved to Backblaze B2) — and **omits five processors you actually use**: Stripe Identity, Sentry, Inngest, Pusher, and Umami. Several are US companies that process EU data, but the policy says nothing about transfers or the legal mechanism (SCCs / EU-US Data Privacy Framework). Naming a vendor you don't use and hiding ones you do is a transparency breach in the EU and a deceptive omission in the US.
**What to do:** Replace R2 with Backblaze B2; add Stripe Identity, Sentry, Inngest, Pusher, Umami; add an "International data transfers" section naming the mechanism per processor; confirm signed DPAs with each.

### 🔴 #10 — Data-rights promises you don't actually deliver. (HIGH — EU)
**Why it matters:** Your data export omits the most sensitive things you hold — service addresses, GPS coordinates, home photos, and chat messages. Your erasure only soft-deletes the user row and bans them in Clerk; it does **not** delete home photos from Backblaze, or purge Stripe/Resend/Sentry. Promising Access/Erasure/Portability and not delivering it is exactly the "promise vs. practice" gap regulators punish.
**What to do:** Expand the export to all personal data held; on erasure, actually delete the B2 home-photo objects and propagate deletion requests downstream (subject to tax-retention carve-outs). Describe the 30-day soft-delete honestly.

These claims appear on your live site/emails and **create liability the moment they aren't literally true.** Each must be either *proven and documented* or *removed/reframed*:

3. **"Eco-certified" / "100% eco-certified" / "we verify the certifications"** — these are self-declared tiers, not third-party certifications. Relabel as self-declared; drop "certified"/"verified."
4. **"Verified by EcoAudit EU"** — a named verifier that may not exist = a *false certification claim*, one of the most serious violations. Remove unless a documented audit relationship is real.
5. **Carbon offset — "offsets 100%" / "neutralise this service"** — needs methodology, the offset provider/registry/standard, tCO2e per booking, and what proportion of the price funds it. Otherwise it's both greenwashing and a "payment-for-nothing" complaint.
6. **"Non-toxic"** — FTC treats this as requiring proof of no health/environmental harm. Replace with specific substantiated attributes.
7. **Affiliate testimonials** ("Anika V. @anika.organises," etc.) — must be genuine, with records, and labelled as paid (#ad) right next to each.

**Rule of thumb:** if you can't hand a regulator a file proving it, don't state it as fact.
